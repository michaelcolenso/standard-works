## SP-01 | Morel
**Morchella spp.** | Rating: **EDIBLE** (must cook)

### 3-Step ID Gate
| Step | Check | Required answer |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Cap surface is a deeply pitted, honeycomb-like mesh — not smooth, wrinkled, brain-like, or saddle-shaped | YES |
| 2 | Cap is fully fused to the stem at its base; slicing vertically reveals a single, continuous hollow cavity from tip to base | YES |
| 3 | Entire mushroom (cap + stem) is completely hollow — no cotton-like fibers, no chambered interior | YES |

**If any answer is NO → do not eat. Photograph and leave.**

### Look-alike Table
| Look-alike | Key danger | Distinguishing feature |
|---|---|---|
| False Morel (*Gyromitra esculenta*) | Contains gyromitrin; converts to rocket-fuel toxin; lethal | Cap wrinkled, brain-like, or lobed — NOT pitted honeycomb; interior chambered |
| Verpas (*Verpa bohemica*, *V. conica*) | GI toxin; less dangerous but real | Cap attached only at top, hangs free at skirt; interior chambered |

### Habitat & Season
Cottonwood and alder riparian corridors, old orchards (near dying apple or elm), logged areas with recent soil disturbance, south-facing burns. Elevation: 0–3,500 ft. Season: March–May; peak April at 500–1,500 ft; May at 2,000–3,500 ft. Follow snow line upslope as season progresses.

### Sustainability Rule
Harvest no more than half of visible fruiting bodies per patch. Leave button-stage and softball-sized or larger specimens. Never rake leaf litter — mycelium is shallow. [HARVEST LIMIT: pending — WA National Forest personal-use limit]

### Prep Note
Must be cooked — raw morels cause GI distress in many individuals; sauté in butter or dry-roast; dehydrate whole for storage up to 1 year.

## SP-02 | Stinging Nettle
**Urtica dioica** | Rating: **EDIBLE with prep**

### 3-Step ID Gate
| Step | Check | Required answer |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Leaves deeply serrated, heart-shaped at base, arranged in **opposite pairs** on the stem | YES |
| 2 | Stem and leaf undersides have visible hollow stinging hairs — bare skin contact causes immediate burning sting | YES |
| 3 | Harvesting only the top 4–6 inches of young growth, before flowers or seeds appear | YES |

**If any answer is NO → do not harvest. (If no sting, it is not this species — confirm before eating.)**

### Look-alike Table
| Look-alike | Key danger | Distinguishing feature |
|---|---|---|
| Deadnettle (*Lamium purpureum*) | None — also edible; lookalike, not dangerous | No stinging hairs; soft, hairy but not irritating; purple-tipped |
| Wood Nettle (*Laportea canadensis*) | Edible when young; not dangerous | Leaves alternate, not opposite; similar sting |

### Habitat & Season
Moist disturbed ground, stream banks, forest edges, river floodplains, garden margins. Abundant throughout PNW lowlands, 0–2,500 ft. Season: February–May for harvest quality; March–April peak before flowering. Post-flower growth is fibrous and inferior.

### Sustainability Rule
Harvest no more than 1/3 of any stand. Cut with scissors — never pull (leaves root system intact for regrowth). Leave all plants below 6 inches.

### Prep Note
Blanch 60 seconds in boiling water or cook thoroughly — stinging deactivated by heat. Excellent in soups, pasta, pesto. Always wear gloves until cooked.

## SP-03 | Ostrich Fern Fiddlehead
**Matteuccia struthiopteris** | Rating: **EDIBLE with prep**

### 3-Step ID Gate
| Step | Check | Required answer |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Coil emerges from a crown (not a single isolated stem), tightly wound, green, with a deep U-shaped groove on the inner face of the stalk | YES |
| 2 | Brown papery scale covering is sparse, easily rubbed away — not dense, woolly, silvery, or clinging heavily | YES |
| 3 | Cross-section of the stalk is U- or C-shaped (grooved), **not round** | YES |

**If any answer is NO → do not eat.**

### Look-alike Table
| Look-alike | Key danger | Distinguishing feature |
|---|---|---|
| Bracken Fern (*Pteridium aquilinum*) | Contains ptaquiloside, a carcinogen; chronic consumption risk | 3-parted frond (clover-like) when uncoiling; round stalk; dry open habitats |
| Lady Fern (*Athyrium filix-femina*) | Edibility uncertain; GI distress reported | Dense, silvery scales that cling; rounder stalk; smaller coil |

### Habitat & Season
Moist streambanks, floodplains, wet ditches, lowland riparian forest. Typically at 0–1,500 ft. Season: March–May; harvest window is brief — 1 to 2 weeks per patch. Coils must be 1–4 inches tall and tightly wound; uncoiling fronds are too fibrous.

### Sustainability Rule
Harvest no more than 1/3 of coils from any crown. Leave the largest and smallest coils. Never strip a crown bare — the plant may not recover.

### Prep Note
Must be cooked — raw fiddleheads cause GI illness. Boil 10–15 minutes, or steam 12 minutes; sauté after cooking. Discard cooking water.

## SP-04 | Oyster Mushroom
**Pleurotus ostreatus** | Rating: **EDIBLE**

### 3-Step ID Gate
| Step | Check | Required answer |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Cap is shelf-like or fan-shaped, growing laterally from dead or dying wood — not from soil | YES |
| 2 | Gills are white, closely spaced, and run down onto a very short or absent stem (decurrent gills) | YES |
| 3 | No ring, no volva (cup at base), flesh smells pleasant and mild — never strongly fishy, rotten, or chemical | YES |

**If any answer is NO → do not eat.**

### Look-alike Table
| Look-alike | Key danger | Distinguishing feature |
|---|---|---|
| Angel Wings (*Pleurocybella porrigens*) | Implicated in fatalities (kidney disease patients); mechanism unclear | Pure white, very thin fragile flesh; grows only on conifers; gills do not run down a stub |
| Mock Oyster (*Phyllotopsis nidulans*) | Causes GI distress | Bright yellow-orange cap; smells strongly unpleasant (cat urine / rotten cheese) |

### Habitat & Season
Dead or dying alder, cottonwood, bigleaf maple; also Douglas-fir and hemlock. Lowland to mid-elevation, 0–3,000 ft. Season: October–May; cold-weather species. Spring flush common March–April after sustained rain. Grows in clusters.

### Sustainability Rule
Cut at substrate level — never pull. Leave at least 20% of any cluster. Do not damage the log surface; mycelium re-fruits from same substrate for multiple seasons.

### Prep Note
Sauté over high heat until edges crisp. Excellent roasted, in soups, or dried. Mild flavor absorbs seasoning readily.

## SP-05 | Miner's Lettuce
**Claytonia perfoliata** | Rating: **EDIBLE**

### 3-Step ID Gate
| Step | Check | Required answer |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | A single rounded or oval leaf completely surrounds the flowering stem, forming a disc through which the stem grows | YES |
| 2 | Plant is small and low-growing (2–12 inches); leaves are smooth, bright green, succulent in texture | YES |
| 3 | Basal leaves (separate, spoon-shaped) are also present at the base of the plant | YES (when plant is young) |

**Note: The perfoliate disc leaf is diagnostic and unique — no dangerous species shares this characteristic.**

### Look-alike Table
| Look-alike | Key danger | Distinguishing feature |
|---|---|---|
| Siberian Miner's Lettuce (*C. sibirica*) | None — also edible | Perfoliate leaf less perfectly round; pink flowers; equally good eating |

No dangerous look-alikes in PNW.

### Habitat & Season
Moist disturbed ground, roadsides, forest edges, garden beds. Abundant throughout PNW lowlands, 0–2,000 ft. Season: February–May; peak March–April before bolting. Flavor turns bitter after flowering; harvest before flowers fully open.

### Sustainability Rule
Cut leaves; never pull roots. Harvest no more than 1/3 of any stand. Highly abundant — avoid overharvesting only in small urban patches.

### Prep Note
Excellent raw in salads; mild, slightly succulent flavor. Can be lightly wilted in a hot pan. No prep required.

## SP-06 | Chickweed
**Stellaria media** | Rating: **EDIBLE**

### 3-Step ID Gate
| Step | Check | Required answer |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Stem has a single line of fine hairs running along one side, switching sides at each node | YES |
| 2 | Leaves are small, oval, smooth, in opposite pairs; lower leaves on short stalks, upper leaves stalkless | YES |
| 3 | No milky white sap when stem is broken | YES |

**The rotating hair line on the stem is diagnostic. If no hair line is visible → confirm species before eating.**

### Look-alike Table
| Look-alike | Key danger | Distinguishing feature |
|---|---|---|
| Scarlet Pimpernel (*Anagallis arvensis*) | Mildly toxic; GI distress | Flowers orange or red; no hair line on stem |
| Spurges (*Euphorbia* spp.) | Toxic; skin irritant | Milky white sap when any part is broken |

### Habitat & Season
Moist garden soil, disturbed ground, forest edges. Year-round in mild PNW lowlands. Best season: February–May and September–November. Avoid in summer heat — becomes bitter; large quantities may cause mild GI upset.

### Sustainability Rule
Abundant garden weed; harvest freely in areas where it grows densely. Cut with scissors; do not pull.

### Prep Note
Raw in salads or sandwiches; mild, slightly grassy flavor. Lightly sautéed. Eat in moderate quantities — very large amounts may cause mild GI upset in some individuals.

## SP-07 | Wood Sorrel
**Oxalis oregana** | Rating: **EDIBLE** (small quantities)

### 3-Step ID Gate
| Step | Check | Required answer |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Leaves trifoliate — 3 heart-shaped leaflets (shamrock-like) that fold downward at night or in bright light | YES |
| 2 | Tiny amount placed on tongue tastes distinctly sour (oxalic acid) | YES |
| 3 | Leaflets are smooth and green (may have purple tint); no spots | YES |

**No dangerous look-alikes in PNW. Sour taste is confirmatory.**

### Look-alike Table
No dangerous look-alikes. Other *Oxalis* species present in PNW (*O. stricta*, *O. corniculata*) are similarly edible and sour.

### Habitat & Season
Moist shaded forest floors, especially under Douglas-fir and Western Redcedar. Common throughout PNW at 0–3,500 ft. Season: March–June peak; present year-round in mild areas.

### Sustainability Rule
Harvest lightly — never strip a patch. Take a handful at most per area; plants are small and carpet-forming; overharvesting damages the patch visually and ecologically.

### Prep Note
Use raw as trail snack or salad garnish; tart, lemony flavor makes an excellent acid substitute. High oxalic acid — use in small quantities only. Do not eat in large amounts (calcium absorption effect; avoid if prone to kidney stones).

## SP-08 | Spring King Bolete
**Boletus edulis** (sensu lato) | Rating: **EDIBLE**

### 3-Step ID Gate
| Step | Check | Required answer |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Cap brown to tan, smooth; pore surface white to pale yellow — never red, orange, or pink | YES |
| 2 | Stem stout, often barrel-shaped at base, with fine white net-like reticulation (raised mesh pattern) on the upper portion | YES |
| 3 | Flesh white or pale when cut — does NOT turn blue or green within 5 minutes of cutting | YES |

**Blue staining of cut flesh = possible toxic look-alike. If any staining occurs → do not eat.**

### Look-alike Table
| Look-alike | Key danger | Distinguishing feature |
|---|---|---|
| Bitter Bolete (*Tylopilus felleus*) | Extremely bitter; not toxic but inedible; ruins dishes | Pore surface pink to rose; stem reticulation dark and coarse; intensely bitter taste |
| Satan's Bolete (*Rubroboletus satanas*) | Toxic; GI distress | Pore surface bright red; base of stem red; flesh blues immediately when cut |

### Habitat & Season
Mixed conifer forest; often near hemlock, Sitka spruce, and white fir. Spring flush at 500–2,500 ft after snowmelt. Season: May–June (spring), second flush September–October at higher elevation. Look for mycorrhizal partners (hemlock, spruce) as host-tree indicator.

### Sustainability Rule
Harvest no more than 50% of visible fruiting bodies per patch. Leave button-stage and any specimens past prime. Slice stem base cleanly; do not disturb duff. [HARVEST LIMIT: pending — WA/OR National Forest NTFP personal-use limit]

### Prep Note
Excellent raw (thinly sliced), sautéed, or dried. Remove spongy pore surface on older specimens. Pairs with cream sauces, pasta, risotto; dries to 10× concentrated flavor.

## SP-09 | Dandelion
**Taraxacum officinale** | Rating: **EDIBLE** (all parts)

### 3-Step ID Gate
| Step | Check | Required answer |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Leaves form a basal rosette directly from the ground; leaves deeply toothed (runcinate), pointing backward toward the base | YES |
| 2 | Hollow stem exudes white milky sap when broken; single yellow composite flower head per stem | YES |
| 3 | Growing in an unsprayed location (no pesticide/herbicide use) | YES |

**Note: Dandelion is one of the most identifiable plants in the PNW. Primary concern is contamination, not misidentification.**

### Look-alike Table
| Look-alike | Key danger | Distinguishing feature |
|---|---|---|
| Cat's Ear (*Hypochaeris radicata*) | None — also edible | Branching stems (not single); leaves hairy; smaller flowers |
| Hawkweeds (*Hieracium* spp.) | None — also edible | Branching stems; hairy leaves; flowers smaller; no milky sap in all species |

No dangerous look-alikes in PNW.

### Habitat & Season
Lawns, roadsides, meadows, garden edges, disturbed ground. Year-round; best spring (March–May) before flower stalks fully develop. Young leaves least bitter; roots most bitter in spring; flowers in April–May.

### Sustainability Rule
Harvest only from unsprayed areas. Abundant; harvest freely. Leave roots intact when harvesting leaves; dig roots intentionally only when needed.

### Prep Note
Young leaves raw in salad (bitter; balance with sweet dressing); older leaves sautéed with garlic (blanch first to reduce bitterness). Flowers raw or battered and fried. Roots roasted as coffee substitute. All parts edible; bitterness increases with age.

## SP-10 | Nodding Onion
**Allium cernuum** | Rating: **EDIBLE**

### 3-Step ID Gate
| Step | Check | Required answer |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | All parts of the plant smell strongly and distinctly of onion or garlic when any part is crushed or broken | YES |
| 2 | Leaves are grass-like, flat or slightly keeled, growing from the base (not alternating on a stem) | YES |
| 3 | Flower head (if present) nods or droops downward at the tip of the stem | YES |

**CRITICAL: Any wild allium-looking plant that does NOT smell of onion or garlic is potentially toxic. If no onion smell → do not eat.**

### Look-alike Table
| Look-alike | Key danger | Distinguishing feature |
|---|---|---|
| Death Camas (*Anticlea elegans*, formerly *Zigadenus*) | Lethal; contains zygacine | No onion or garlic smell — this is the single, absolute distinguishing feature |
| Crow Poison (*Nothoscordum bivalve*) | Toxic | No onion smell |

### Habitat & Season
Rocky slopes, open meadows, dry grassy hillsides, forest openings. Elevation: 500–6,000 ft. Season: May–July for aerial parts; bulbs available September–October. Spring leaves March–May at lower elevations.

### Sustainability Rule
Harvest no more than 1/4 of any visible population. Bulbs are slow-growing; prefer harvesting leaves over bulbs. Leave all flowering plants in small populations.

### Prep Note
Use exactly as cultivated onion or garlic — flavor is excellent, slightly more pungent. Raw in salads, cooked in any savory dish. Leaves and bulbs both edible; dry bulbs for storage.

## SP-11 | Dryad's Saddle
**Cerioporus squamosus** (formerly *Polyporus squamosus*) | Rating: **EDIBLE** (young only)

### 3-Step ID Gate
| Step | Check | Required answer |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Large, fan-shaped or saddle-shaped bracket fruiting from dead or dying hardwood (elm, maple, cottonwood, ash); cap surface has dark brown scales arranged in concentric rings on a cream-tan background | YES |
| 2 | Underside has large, angular, cream-colored pores (not gills, not tiny round pores) — pore openings are clearly visible without a lens | YES |
| 3 | Specimen is young and firm (not old, soggy, or insect-riddled); flesh smells pleasantly of fresh watermelon rind or cucumber | YES |

**Edibility is age-dependent. Mature/old specimens become tough and unpalatable; harvest young only.**

### Look-alike Table
No dangerous look-alikes in PNW. Other large bracket polypores with different characteristics are generally not dangerous but confirm pore surface and host wood before eating.

### Habitat & Season
Dead and dying hardwoods — elm, bigleaf maple, cottonwood, occasionally fruit trees. Lowland PNW, 0–2,000 ft. Season: April–June (primary spring flush); may appear again October–November after fall rains.

### Sustainability Rule
Harvest only young, firm specimens. Cut at base — do not peel from wood. Leave any colony where more than 50% of specimens are old or insect-damaged.

### Prep Note
Slice thin and sauté or roast young specimens; tender parts near the edges of young brackets are best. Flavor mild, similar to oyster mushroom; toughens with cooking time. Suitable for pickling.

## SP-12 | Garlic Mustard
**Alliaria petiolata** | Rating: **EDIBLE** (invasive — harvest encouraged)

### 3-Step ID Gate
| Step | Check | Required answer |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Leaves are broadly heart-shaped to triangular, with scalloped or toothed edges, growing alternately on the stem | YES |
| 2 | Crushed leaves emit a distinct garlic odor | YES |
| 3 | Flowers (if present) are small, white, 4-petaled, arranged in a cluster at stem tip | YES |

**Garlic odor when crushed is the key confirmatory feature. No dangerous species in PNW shares this combination.**

### Look-alike Table
| Look-alike | Key danger | Distinguishing feature |
|---|---|---|
| Hairy Bittercress (*Cardamine hirsuta*) | None — also edible | No garlic smell; smaller plant; pinnate leaves (divided) |

No dangerous look-alikes in PNW.

### Habitat & Season
Forest edges, roadsides, disturbed moist ground, urban parks. Invasive throughout PNW lowlands. Season: March–May for best eating (first-year basal rosette and young second-year shoots); seed pods appear May–June. Land managers actively encourage harvest.

### Sustainability Rule
Invasive species — there is no sustainability concern for overharvesting. Pull entire plant including root where possible; this removes the plant from the ecosystem. Dispose of seed pods in trash, not compost, if present.

### Prep Note
Leaves and roots edible raw; mild garlic-mustard flavor. Use in pesto, as salad green, sautéed as a potherb. Flavor intensifies with cooking; use in moderation as a flavoring agent.
